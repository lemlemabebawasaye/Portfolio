Predicting and Understanding Emergency Shutdown Durations Level of Pipeline Incidents Using Machine Learning Models and Explainable AI

Lemlem Asaye, Chau Le, Ying Huang, Trung Q. Le, Om Prakash Yadav, and Tuyen Le

MDPI Processes, 2025

This study examines pipeline incident risks by predicting emergency shutdown durations using machine learning. Random Forest and Light Gradient Boosting Machine (LightGBM) were applied to PHMSA data (2010–2022), capturing complex patterns beyond traditional methods. LightGBM achieved 75.0% accuracy, with Random Forest close at 74.8%. Explainable AI techniques, like SHAP values, identified key factors affecting shutdowns, including pipeline material, age, and layout. These insights aid pipeline operators and regulators in making data-driven decisions to enhance safety and reduce disruptions.

Insights into the Interactions of Pipeline Risk Factors and Consequences Using Association Rule Mining

Lemlem Asaye, Chau Le, Trung Q. Le, Om Prakash Yadav, and Tuyen Le

ASCE Journal of Performance of Constructed Facilities, December 16,, 2024

This study examines pipeline incidents that disrupt global energy transport, emphasizing the limitations of past research, which typically used simple statistical methods or relied heavily on expert opinions. By employing association rule mining on data from the Pipeline Hazardous Material Safety Administration, this research identifies complex relationships among incident causes, pipeline features, failure types, and shutdown periods. Results show that combinations of factors, such as older, large-diameter pipelines located underground, increase the likelihood of mechanical damage and longer shutdowns due to excavation incidents. These insights are valuable for improving pipeline planning, design, and operational safety, highlighting the need for targeted prevention strategies to ensure reliable energy transportation.

Role of coarse aggregate sizes in evaluating the bond-slip mechanism of reinforced concrete

Lemlem Asaye, Wajahat Sammer Ansari, Esayas Gebreyouhannes, Zahoor Hussain, Ahsan Naseem

Construction and Building Materials, December 25,, 2024

This study investigates how the size of coarse aggregates affects the bond between steel reinforcement and concrete, which is crucial for the strength of reinforced concrete structures, particularly at beam-column joints. Using pullout tests, the research compares aggregate sizes from 9.5 mm to 37.5 mm and also tests pure mortar samples. Results indicate that smaller aggregates significantly reduce bond strength, with mortar achieving only 38.65% of the strength compared to concrete with 25 mm aggregates. Existing bond prediction models perform well for larger aggregates but are less accurate for smaller sizes. This research underscores the importance of considering aggregate size in structural design and suggests further study on aggregate shape and moisture content to enhance bond strength predictions.

Natural Language Processing for Infrastructure Resilience to Natural Disasters: A Scientometric Review

Muhammad Ali Moriyani, Lemlem Asaye , Chau Le, Tuyen Le, Trung Le

This study explores the role of natural language processing (NLP) in strengthening infrastructure resilience against natural disasters. While NLP has shown promise in analyzing unstructured data for emergency decision-making, such as detecting infrastructure damage, research on its broader applications remains limited. To address this gap, this study conducts a scientometric review using bibliographic records from Scopus and Web of Science. The analysis identifies key trends, developments, challenges, and future opportunities in NLP-based infrastructure resilience research. By mapping the global research landscape, this study enhances understanding of NLP’s potential to improve disaster response and infrastructure protection.

A Zero Shot Detection Based Approach for Fish Species Recognition in Underwater Environments

Lemlem Asaye , Muhammad Ali Moriyani, Chau Le, Tuyen Le

American Society of Civil Engineers, Journal of Management in Engineering, October 26 , 2023

This study investigates bid collusion in highway project procurement, where competitive bidding aims to secure the lowest project costs but can be compromised by collusive arrangements. Traditional detection methods rely on engineers’ estimates and bid cost similarities, which experienced colluders can manipulate. To address these limitations, this research introduces a data-driven Collusion Red-Flag Detection (CRFD) framework using pattern mining and statistical analysis. The framework identifies contractors with unusual winning patterns, particularly when specific bidders are present. Additionally, it integrates influential factors to improve detection accuracy. This approach provides project owners with an effective tool for early collusion detection, supporting better decision-making in awarding contracts.

Network theory–based approach to data-driven assessment of bidding competition in highway construction

Muhammad Ali Moriyani, Lemlem Asaye , Chau Le, Tuyen Le

American Society of Civil Engineers, Journal of Management in Engineering, October 05 , 2023

This work introduces a deep-learning fish detection and classification model with the Convolutional Block Attention Module (CBAM) for enhanced detection. It employs a cost-efficient Deep Active Learning approach to select informative samples from unlabeled data. Probabilistic modeling, using mixture density networks, estimates probability distributions for localization and classification.

Optimizing and Gauging Model Performance with Metrics to Integrate with Existing Video Surveys

Jack Prior, Simegnew Yihunie Alaba, Farron Wallace, Matthew D. Campbell, Chiranjibi Shah, M. M. Nabi, Paul F. Mickle, Robert Moorhead, John E. Ball

IEEE Ocean Conference & Exposition Biloxi, MS, USA, September 25--28, 2023

Automated deep-learning models offer efficient fish population monitoring in baited underwater video sampling. Precise results are essential, and otolith age-reader comparison helps evaluate model performance. Increasing annotations improve most species' performance, but challenges remain with occlusion, turbidity, schooling species, and cryptic appearances. Red Snapper's multiple-year evaluation highlights location, time, and environmental considerations. Adapting active learning algorithms can enhance model efficiency. These quality assessment methods aid in integrating automated methods with manual video counts.

Class-Aware Fish Species Recognition Using Deep Learning for an Imbalanced Dataset

Simegnew Yihunie Alaba, John E. Ball

MDPI Sensing and Imaging , 2022

This work tackles fish species recognition using object detection to handle multiple fish in a single image. The model combines MobileNetv3-large and VGG16 backbones with an SSD detection head and proposes a class-aware loss function to address the class imbalance.

WCNN3D: Wavelet Convolutional Neural Network-Based 3D Object Detection for Autonomous Driving

Simegnew Yihunie Alaba, John E. Ball

MDPI Sensing and Imaging , 2022

This work introduces a 3D object detection network without pooling operations, using wavelet-multiresolution analysis and frequency filters for enhanced feature extraction. The model incorporates discrete wavelet transforms (DWT) and inverse wavelet transforms (IWT) with skip connections to enrich feature representation and recover lost details during downsampling. Experimental results demonstrate improved performance compared to PointPillars, with reduced trainable parameters.

SEAMAPD21: a large-scale reef fish dataset for fine-grained categorization

Océane Boulais, Simegnew Yihunie Alaba John E Ball, Matthew Campbell, Ahmed Tashfin Iftekhar, Robert Moorehead, James Primrose, Jack Prior, Farron Wallace, Henry Yu, Aotian Zheng

IEEE CVPR FGVC8: The Eight Workshop on Fine-Grained Visual Categorization, 2021

We introduce SEAMAPD21, a large-scale reef fish dataset from the Gulf of Mexico, vital for fishery monitoring. This dataset enables efficient automated analysis of fish species, overcoming previous challenges. SEAMAPD21 includes 90,000 annotations for 130 fish species from 2018 and 2019 surveys. We present baseline experiments and results, available at https://github.com/SEFSC/SEAMAPD21.
