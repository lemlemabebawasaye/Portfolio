This study explores the role of natural language processing (NLP) in strengthening infrastructure resilience against natural disasters. To address this gap, this study conducts a scientometric review using bibliographic records from Scopus and Web of Science. The analysis identifies key trends, developments, challenges, and future opportunities in NLP-based infrastructure resilience research. By mapping the global research landscape, this study enhances understanding of NLP‚Äôs potential to improve disaster response and infrastructure protection.

Detecting red-flag bidding patterns in low-bid procurement for highway projects with pattern mining

Lemlem Asaye , Muhammad Ali Moriyani, Chau Le, Tuyen Le

American Society of Civil Engineers, Journal of Management in Engineering, October 26 , 2023

This study investigates bid collusion in highway project procurement, where competitive bidding aims to secure the lowest project costs but can be compromised by collusive arrangements. Traditional detection methods rely on engineers‚Äô estimates and bid cost similarities, which experienced colluders can manipulate. To address these limitations, this research introduces a data-driven Collusion Red-Flag Detection framework using pattern mining and statistical analysis. The framework identifies contractors with unusual winning patterns, particularly when specific bidders are present. Additionally, it integrates influential factors to improve detection accuracy. This approach provides project owners with an effective tool for early collusion detection, supporting better decision-making in awarding contracts.

Network theory‚Äìbased approach to data-driven assessment of bidding competition in highway construction

Muhammad Ali Moriyani, Lemlem Asaye, Chau Le, Tuyen Le

American Society of Civil Engineers, Journal of Management in Engineering, October 05 , 2023

This study examines competitive bidding in design-bid-build highway construction contracts, highlighting the limitations of traditional evaluation methods that focus solely on the number of bidders. It introduces a data-driven network theory approach to assess competition from the perspective of project owners. By modeling competition as a bidirected network based on past winning and losing records, the study develops new centrality measures to evaluate bidder competitiveness. These measures show stronger correlations with bidding performance than the traditional bidder count. The findings provide state highway agencies with a more comprehensive understanding of competition, supporting improved decision-making in project bidding processes.

Insights from Applying Association Rule Mining to Pipeline Incident Report Data

Lemlem Asaye , Muhammad Ali Moriyani, Chau Le, Trung Q. Le, Om Prakash Yadav

American Society of Civil Engineers, Computing in Civil Engineering 2023, January 25, 2024

This study examines pipeline incidents that have caused shutdowns and economic losses, emphasizing the need to understand their causes and impacts. Using data from the Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration (PHMSA), this research applies association rule mining to uncover relationships between incident causes, background factors such as materials, and shutdown durations. Unlike previous studies that relied on simple statistical analyses, this approach provides deeper insights into how multiple factors interact. The findings are expected to contribute to better pipeline planning, design, and operations by identifying key risk factors and informing preventive measures to enhance infrastructure reliability.

Survival Analysis for Pipeline Longevity and Maintenance Optimization

Lemlem Asaye, Chau Le, Trung Q. Le, Om Prakash Yadav, and Tuyen Le

American Society of Civil Engineers, Computing in Civil Engineering 2025, January 25, 2025

This study addresses the risks of aging pipelines and the limitations of traditional risk models that ignore cumulative failures. Using recurrent event survival analysis with Prentice, Williams, and Peterson models, it examines incident data from PHMSA and network attributes from USEIA. Findings show survival probabilities drop sharply in the first year, with 35.66% of failures linked to equipment defects and operational errors. Subsequent failures accelerate deterioration, highlighting the need for frequent inspections. The proposed approach provides a more accurate risk assessment and supports proactive maintenance strategies.

Regional Variations in Pipeline Longevity: Insights From a Spatial and Survival Analysis Approach

Lemlem Asaye, Chau Le, Trung Q. Le, Om Prakash Yadav, and Tuyen Le

CSCE Construction Specialty & CRC Conference July 28-31, 2025

This study explores how geographic variations affect pipeline longevity using spatial (Getis-Ord Gi*) and survival analysis (Prentice-Williams-Peterson and Cox Proportional Hazards models). Analyzing PHMSA and USEIA data (2010‚Äì2022), it identifies failure hotspots. The Cox PH model (concordance index: 0.73) shows hotspot pipelines have twice the failure risk (hazard ratio: 2.089), while the Midwest faces a fourfold risk increase. Findings highlight the need for region-specific maintenance and material strategies to improve pipeline reliability.

Experimental Study on the Effect of Stress and Water Content on Resilient Modulus of Unsaturated Subgrade Soil

Amare Mulugeta Damtew, Tompai Zoltan, Lemlem Abebaw Asaye, Yasir Mahmood

Arabian Journal of Geosciences, April 2025 , 2025

This study examines factors affecting the resilient modulus (Mr) of silty sand subgrade soil using triaxial loading tests. The soil, tested in its unsaturated state to reflect site conditions, underwent characterization and cyclic loading tests. Key factors analyzed include deviatoric stress, confining stress, and water content. Results indicate that Mr is highly influenced by these factors, with optimal water content improving shear resistance and elastic deformation under certain stress levels. In contrast, soils outside this range exhibit complete plastic deformation.

Identifying Hotspots in Natural Gas and Oil Pipeline Networks and Understanding Incident Risk Factors: An Integrated Spatial Analysis and Machine Learning Approach‚Äù ASCE Journal of Pipeline Systems Engineering and Practice

Lemlem Asaye, Chau Le, Ying Huang, Trung Q. Le, Om Prakash Yadav, and Tuyen Le

ASCE Journal of Pipeline Systems Engineering and Practice, April 15, 2025

This study analyzes spatial variations in pipeline incident risks using PHMSA and USEIA data. Spatial analysis identified hotspots in California and Ohio, while machine learning models, particularly Random Forest, assessed contributing factors with 82% accuracy for natural gas pipelines and 94% for oil pipelines. Results show natural gas pipelines are prone to operational errors, while oil pipelines face higher corrosion risks. Findings support targeted safety interventions by identifying localized incident clusters more effectively than broad geographic analyses.
